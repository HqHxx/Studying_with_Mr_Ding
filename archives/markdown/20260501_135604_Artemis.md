# Artemis: The Enigmatic Moon Huntress Who Prowled Both Mountains and Myths

# 阿尔忒弥斯：穿梭于群山与神话间的神秘月之猎手

When NASA named its flagship lunar program **Artemis**, it didn’t just pick a random celestial sibling—it tapped into a millennia-old **sign** of female power, wildness, and duality. That we still revere this Greek goddess today is a **phenomenon** that transcends temples and scrolls: a figure who guards **childhood** innocence while delivering **irreparable** justice, who grants **illumination** under the moon’s pale glow yet hunts in shadow, and who remains one of antiquity’s most **beloved**—and most feared—deities.

美国国家航空航天局将其旗舰登月项目命名为阿尔忒弥斯时，并非随意挑选了一位与月球相关的神祇——而是选用了一个承载了数千年历史的女性力量、野性与二元性的象征符号。时至今日，我们依然崇敬这位希腊女神，这一现象早已超越了神庙与卷轴的范畴：她既是守护孩童纯真的存在，也能伸张无可挽回的正义；既能在月色清辉下带来启迪，也会在暗影中狩猎；她仍是古代最受爱戴，也最令人敬畏的神祇之一。

Born to Zeus and Leto on either sun-dappled Delos or **sunbaked** Ortygia near Ephesus’ **seacoast**, Artemis wasted no time claiming her dominion. At three, perched on her father’s lap, she listed her demands with the poise of a ruler drafting a charter, not a toddler: eternal virginity, as many epithets as Apollo, a silver bow forged by the Cyclopes, **towering** mountains to roam, and a single city (she chose Ephesus) as her only human foothold. Zeus, amused by her **enthusiasm** and strategic eye, granted every **boon**—plus a pack of feral hounds from Pan and sixty Oceanids, all of the same age, as her handmaids of the hunt.

阿尔忒弥斯是宙斯与勒托之女，出生在阳光斑驳的提洛岛，或是以弗所海滨被烈日烤晒的奥尔提吉亚岛。她甫一降临便着手确立自己的统治权。三岁时，她坐在父亲的膝头，以统治者起草宪章的沉稳姿态列出了自己的诉求，而非一个孩童的任性：永恒的贞洁、与阿波罗同等数量的别名、独眼巨人打造的银弓、可供漫游的高耸群山，以及一座专属的人类城邦（她选择了以弗所）作为自己仅有的世俗据点。宙斯被她的热忱与战略眼光逗乐，应允了她所有的恩惠——还从潘那里得到了一群野性猎犬，以及六十名同龄的俄刻阿尼得斯神女，作为她狩猎的侍女。

From the start, **distinctive** traits **characterized** Artemis. Unlike Aphrodite, who radiated romantic charm, or Athena, who stood for civic **defense**, she was the **outdoor** goddess par excellence: a guardian of wild forests, **sprouting** **seed** beds, and **village** boundaries where human order frayed into nature’s chaos. She stalked deer and wild boar, her favorite **quarry**, across craggy peaks and marshy water junctions; painted pottery and marble friezes **attest** to this, showing her with a silver bow slung over her shoulder, a **pelt** of lion or panther draped across her shoulders, and a deer or hound at her heels. But she was no mere huntress: as “Mistress of the Animals,” she also protected young creatures, guiding lost fawns to safety and punishing anyone who broke nature’s unwritten **convention**.

阿尔忒弥斯从一开始就有着鲜明的特质。与散发浪漫魅力的阿佛洛狄忒，或是代表城邦防卫的雅典娜不同，她是当之无愧的户外女神：她守护着原始森林、萌芽的苗床，以及人类秩序逐渐融入自然混沌的村庄边界。她在崎岖的山峰与沼泽水畔追踪鹿群与野猪——这是她最爱的猎物；彩绘陶器与大理石饰带都印证了这一点，画面中的她肩挎银弓，身披狮或豹的毛皮，身后跟着鹿或猎犬。但她并非单纯的猎手：作为“万物之主”，她也保护幼崽，将迷路的小鹿引向安全之地，并惩罚任何破坏自然不成文惯例的人。

This duality—nurturer and destroyer—played out repeatedly in myth. When Agamemnon killed one of her sacred stags in a sacred grove and **publicly** bragged of his skill, she becalmed his Achaean fleet off Troy’s **shoreline**, demanding Iphigenia’s sacrifice as atonement. Yet at the last moment, she substituted a stag and spirited the princess away to Tauris as her lifelong priestess, **perpetuating** her own cult while sparing a young life. She also turned Callisto, a favorite Oceanid who had lain with Zeus, into a bear, only to place her and her son Arcas in the night sky as constellations, a strange but lasting gesture of mercy. And when Niobe mocked Leto for having only two children, Artemis and Apollo **enthusiastically** slaughtered all six or seven of Niobe’s daughters, leaving their mother to turn to stone with grief.

这种兼具养育者与毁灭者的二元性在神话中反复上演。当阿伽门农在圣林中射杀了她的一头圣鹿，并公然炫耀自己的技艺时，阿尔忒弥斯让他的亚该亚舰队在特洛伊海岸线外停滞不前，要求以伊菲革涅亚的献祭作为赎罪。但在最后一刻，她用一头鹿代替了祭品，将公主秘密送往陶里斯，让她成为自己终身的女祭司，既延续了自己的崇拜，也救下了年轻的生命。她还将曾与宙斯结合的宠眷俄刻阿尼得斯神女卡利斯托变成了熊，随后又将她与儿子阿尔卡斯置于夜空，化为星座，这是一个怪异却持久的仁慈姿态。而当尼俄伯嘲笑勒托仅育有两个孩子时，阿尔忒弥斯与阿波罗满腔热情地屠戮了尼俄伯的六七个女儿，让她们的母亲悲痛得化为石像。

Artemis’ **heritage** lives on not just in myth but in ruins and art. The **Temple of Artemis at Ephesus**, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, stood as a testament to ancient Greek and eastern **craftsmanship**: 127 Ionic columns, 24-ton architrave blocks locals swore the goddess herself helped place, and a cedar-wood cult statue covered in **plantlike** carvings, bees, and small animals (now thought to represent **quartz** crystals or fertility symbols). Today, only a single, reclaimed column marks the site, a quiet reminder of grandeur lost to **decay**. But other representations endure: the *Diane de Versailles* in the Louvre, a Roman copy of a lost Leochares original where she grasps a stag’s antlers mid-pursuit; and a 2nd-century CE statue in the Vatican, where a crescent moon crowns her head and a hound stands ready to **guide** her next hunt. Even in Attica’s Brauron, where **regular** rites of passage for brides-to-be once took place near a sacred spring, painted libation vessels show girls dancing and running, honoring the goddess who watched over their journey from childhood to womanhood. 阿尔忒弥斯的遗产不仅留存于神话之中，也见于遗迹与艺术之中。以弗所的阿尔忒弥斯神庙是古代世界七大奇迹之一，它见证了古希腊与东方的精湛工艺：127根爱奥尼柱式、当地人声称由女神亲自协助安放的24吨重楣梁块，以及覆盖着植物雕刻、蜜蜂与小型动物的雪松木质崇拜雕像（如今被认为代表石英晶体或生育象征）。如今，仅存一根被重新发掘的柱石标记着遗址所在地，无声地提醒着人们因腐朽而消逝的宏伟。但其他的艺术形象仍留存于世：卢浮宫的《凡尔赛的狄安娜》是一件失传的莱奥卡雷斯原作的罗马复制品，画面中的她正追逐时握住鹿的犄角；梵蒂冈的一尊公元2世纪雕像中，她头戴新月冠，身旁猎犬正准备引领她开启下一场狩猎。即使在阿提卡的布拉伦——这里曾有专为准新娘举办的定期成年礼，临近一处圣泉——彩绘奠酒器皿上仍绘有女孩们跳舞奔跑的场景，纪念着这位守护她们从孩童迈向成年旅程的女神。（字数：792）
